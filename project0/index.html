Author: Caralyn Liu

Part 1: Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Analysis

In the 24mm wide-angle shot, the camera is extremely close. This proximity exaggerates the relative distance between facial features. The nose, being closest to the lens, appears disproportionately large, while the ears and cheeks recede, creating a distorted effect.

For the 120mm telephoto shot, the photographer moved back and zoomed in. By increasing the physical distance, the relative depths of the facial features become negligible, minimizing distortion. The long focal length then narrows the field of view, creating perspective compression. This flattens the features into a more proportional and flattering arrangement.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Analysis

These images of Berkeley's Campanile illustrate the powerful effect of focal length and camera position on spatial representation. The first image, taken from a distance with a long focal length, exhibits strong perspective compression. The physical space between the foreground tree and the background tower appears collapsed. This "flattening" effect minimizes the sense of depth, making the distant Campanile feel more immediate and integrated with the foreground elements. Conversely, the second image was captured from a closer position with a short focal length (wide angle). This technique exaggerates perspective, creating a pronounced sense of depth. The wide field of view and the leading lines of the road draw the viewer into the scene, emphasizing the distance to the tower and making the space feel more expansive and three-dimensional.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Process

This dolly zoom was created using a stuffed cat as the main subject, placed on a kitchen countertop against a background of blue tiles. While the main subject stays static, the background appears to warp and compress. As the focal length increases, the field of view narrows. This causes the blue tiles in the background to magnify, making each tile appear larger. Consequently, fewer tiles are visible in the frame, creating the illusion that the background is closing in on the subject.
